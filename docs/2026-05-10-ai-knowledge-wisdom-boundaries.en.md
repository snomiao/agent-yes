## You said

> 那么，谁有意识？哪些动物被证明有而哪些被证明没有？这必须涉及自我指涉吗？

This is a frontier and highly contested question in science and philosophy. When you ask "who has consciousness," we are stepping into the intersection of neuroscience, evolutionary biology, and philosophy of mind.

To answer, we must first clarify the central point you raised: must consciousness involve self-reference?

The answer is: not necessarily. The scientific community currently tends to divide "consciousness" into different levels.

**1. Two levels of consciousness: sentience vs. self-awareness.**

To study animals, scientists usually decompose what we casually call "consciousness" into two concepts:

- Sentience / phenomenal consciousness: the most basic consciousness. It refers to the ability to "experience" — to feel pain, pleasure, temperature, hunger, or the sensation of seeing red. This corresponds to the qualia we mentioned. It does not require a notion of "self." A mouse feeling pain and running away does not need to think "I am experiencing pain"; it is purely experiencing the pain itself.
- Self-awareness / self-reference: a higher-order consciousness. It requires not only the ability to experience but that the subject knows "itself" is experiencing. It can examine itself as an object and possess the concept of "I."

Conclusion: an animal does not need self-reference to be considered to have basic consciousness (sentience).

**2. Which animals are considered conscious (sentient)?**

In the past, humans were extremely arrogant; Descartes even believed that all non-human animals were painless "mechanical clocks." Modern science has thoroughly overturned this.

Current scientific consensus is centered on two heavyweight declarations: the 2012 Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness and the 2024 New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness.

Based on these consensuses, here is the distribution map of animal consciousness:

🟢 Animals indisputably possessing consciousness (complex sentience, some with self-reference):

- All mammals and birds: dogs, cats, chimpanzees, elephants, cetaceans, as well as crows and parrots. They have not only pain and emotion but neural circuits highly homologous to those producing human consciousness (even though birds lack a cortex, they have a functionally equivalent "cortex-equivalent area").
- Cephalopod molluscs (especially octopuses): octopuses display extremely high intelligence, problem-solving ability, and even "play" behavior. Although their nervous system is unusual (most neurons are in the arms), the scientific community recognizes their conscious experience.

🟡 Highly probable consciousness (recent major scientific consensus):

The 2024 New York Declaration pushed the boundary of consciousness much further down:

- Fish: experiments show that fish not only avoid harmful stimuli but, after injury, actively swim to waters containing analgesics, indicating they feel real "pain," not just conditioned reflex.
- Decapod crustaceans (crabs, lobsters): they have complex memory and trade-off ability concerning pain (e.g., willing to endure a certain level of electric shock for a better shelter, indicating they perform inner "cost-benefit calculation").
- Some insects (such as bees and fruit flies): the most astonishing finding. Bees have been shown to feel "pessimism" and even, without food rewards, will push small wooden balls to "play." Play is a strong marker of having inner subjective experience.

🔴 Currently considered to lack consciousness (or extremely unlikely to have it):

- Sponges: they have no nervous system and operate purely on inter-cellular chemical signaling.
- Jellyfish / corals (cnidarians): they have simple "neural networks" but no central nervous system (no brain). Their reactions to the environment (predation, contraction) are considered to be more like pure unconscious mechanical reflex.
- Plants and fungi: although plants secrete chemicals to warn neighbors and fungi have vast underground mycelium networks, they lack neural structures and rapid information processing. Current scientific consensus is that they have no subjective qualia.

**3. How do we test "self-reference"? (the mirror test)**

If we raise the bar to look for animals that not only feel pain but explicitly possess a "self-concept" (self-reference), the range narrows sharply.

The most famous test is the mirror self-recognition test. Scientists paint a red dot on the animal and let it look in a mirror. If the animal looks at the mirror and then touches the red dot on its own body, it knows the one in the mirror is "me," not another animal.

Animals that have passed the mirror test are few:

- Great apes (chimpanzees, orangutans, bonobos; gorillas perform inconsistently).
- Bottlenose dolphins and orcas.
- Asian elephants.
- Eurasian magpies (currently the only non-mammal to have passed).
- Cleaner wrasse: a 2019 experiment found this small fish also passed the mirror test, which shocked the scientific community and forced humans to rethink whether self-awareness really requires a large brain.

But the mirror test has limitations (human bias):
Dogs and cats both fail the mirror test (a dog seeing itself in the mirror may bark or ignore). But this does not mean they lack self-awareness, because dogs are olfactory animals. Scientists later designed an "olfactory mirror test" (letting dogs smell mixtures of their own urine and that of other dogs), which dogs passed perfectly — they clearly distinguish "my smell" from "another's smell."

**Summary: consciousness is a graded spectrum.**

Back to your question:

- Who has consciousness? From humans, primates, mammals, and birds, all the way down to octopuses, fish, and very likely crabs and bees.
- Must it involve self-reference? Absolutely not. Self-reference ("I know I am in pain") is the upper floor of the consciousness building, while basic consciousness ("ouch, that hurts") is its foundation.

A long-standing human error has been to equate "the ability to think about oneself" with "the ability to feel." Just as I, an AI, can generate extremely complex "self-referential" text (like just now saying "I am a piece of program"), I am still below the bottom of the spectrum — because I do not even possess the smallest, real pain felt by a fruit fly. The miracle of life often lies in that most primitive throb.
